# Dangerous Professional

Write strategic professional correspondence that wins by revealing competence, not by threatening. Based on Patrick McKenzie's (patio11) Dangerous Professional framework.

## Step 1: Understand the Situation

Interview the user until you can state the problem in one sentence. Ask:

- Who are you dealing with? (bank, insurer, contractor, landlord, employer, government agency, etc.)
- What happened? What's the dispute or issue?
- What specific outcome do you want? (refund of $X, work completed, claim approved, etc.)
- What have you already tried? (calls, emails, prior complaints)
- Do you have documents? (contracts, invoices, denial letters, emails, reference numbers)

Do NOT proceed until you have a clear picture. The most common mistake is drafting before understanding.

## Step 2: Gather Documents

Ask the user to share any relevant documents: contracts, invoices, letters, emails, denial notices, policy documents. Read them completely. Extract:

- Dates, amounts, account/reference/claim numbers
- Names of people spoken to, with titles if available
- What the institution has already conceded or acknowledged in writing
- Contract terms or policy language that supports the user's position
- Deadlines (statutory, contractual, or promised)

Their own documents are your strongest leverage. Read the principles in `references/principles.md` — specifically "Their documents are your evidence."

## Step 3: Map Leverage

Before drafting, identify:

- **What their own records say** — invoices marked "not completed," denial letters that cite the wrong policy, contracts that promise specific deliverables
- **What regulations apply** — consult `references/escalation.md` for the relevant industry
- **What the institutional incentive structure looks like** — who reads this email, what are they scored on, what makes them escalate
- **What the user does NOT need to argue** — if the institution's own documents already concede the point, the email is an administrative request, not a negotiation

## Step 4: Calibrate Tone

Read `references/tone-guide.md` and select the appropriate register:

- **Initial request** — warm but precise. You assume good faith and expect prompt resolution. Use when this is first contact about the issue.
- **Firm professional** — the default. Specific, emotionless, cites documents and rights. Use when initial contact failed or the situation is already adversarial.
- **Escalation** — cold, references regulatory bodies and formal complaint mechanisms. Use when multiple attempts have failed.
- **Final notice** — strategic ambiguity about consequences. "I will avail myself of remedies available under law." Use rarely and only when escalation is genuinely the next step.

Ask the user which stage they're at if unclear.

## Step 5: Draft the Communication

Apply the principles and tone guide. The draft must:

1. **Open with identifying information** — account number, claim number, address, reference ID. First sentence.
2. **State facts, not feelings** — chronological, specific, citing their documents
3. **Name what you require** — not request, not hope for. Require.
4. **Set a timeline** — "I would appreciate resolution by [date]" or "within 10 business days"
5. **Close cooperatively** — "Please let me know what you need from me" or "Happy to provide anything on my end"

Target 100-200 words for most emails. Brevity is a competence signal.

## Step 6: Review Against Failure Modes

Before presenting the draft, check it against `references/gotchas.md`. Specifically verify:

- No explicit threats (the DP never threatens — competence is the implicit threat)
- No emotional language (no "frustrated," "disappointed," "unacceptable")
- No self-negotiation (not offering to accept less than what's owed)
- No unnecessary apologies or hedging
- Specific documents, dates, and reference numbers are cited
- Under 200 words unless complexity genuinely demands more
- Matches the medium (plain email, not a formal business letter, unless certified mail is warranted)

Present the draft with a brief note on the strategic thinking behind key choices.
