# Escalation Paths by Industry

When a Dangerous Professional communication doesn't resolve the issue, these are the escalation paths by industry. Reference the appropriate regulatory body to signal competence — and file with them when warranted.

## Banking & Financial Services

- **CFPB (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau)** — consumerfinance.gov/complaint — the regulator banks fear most for consumer matters. Complaints are public, tracked, and banks must respond within 15 days.
- **OCC (Office of the Comptroller of the Currency)** — for national banks and federal savings associations
- **FDIC** — for state-chartered banks that are not Fed members
- **State Banking Commissioner** — varies by state, often faster than federal routes
- **Federal Reserve** — for state-chartered banks that are Fed members
- **Applicable statute**: Truth in Lending Act (TILA), Electronic Fund Transfer Act (EFTA), Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA)

## Credit Bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)

- **CFPB** — consumerfinance.gov/complaint — the primary escalation
- **FTC (Federal Trade Commission)** — reportfraud.ftc.gov
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **Applicable statute**: Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) — provides statutory damages of $100-$1,000 per violation, plus actual damages and attorney's fees. McKenzie notes: "if you report to me that it is verified, that will be a per-se violation of the FCRA"

## Insurance (Health)

- **State Insurance Commissioner/Department of Insurance** — every state has one; this is the primary regulator
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services)** — for Medicare/Medicaid plans
- **Department of Labor** — for employer-sponsored plans (ERISA)
- **Applicable statute**: state insurance codes, ERISA (for employer plans), ACA provisions

## Insurance (Auto/Home/Property)

- **State Department of Insurance** — primary regulator, files are public
- **NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners)** — naic.org — cross-state coordination
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **Applicable statute**: state insurance codes, state unfair claims settlement practices acts

## Contractors & Home Services

- **State Contractor Licensing Board** — most states require licensure; complaints affect license renewal
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **Local Building Inspector/Code Enforcement** — for work quality or permit issues
- **Better Business Bureau** — lower leverage but creates a public record
- **Applicable statute**: state consumer protection acts, state home improvement acts (vary by state), mechanic's lien statutes

## Landlords & Housing

- **Local Housing Authority** — for habitability, code violations
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **HUD (Department of Housing and Urban Development)** — for discrimination (Fair Housing Act)
- **Local Tenant Rights Organizations** — often provide free legal assistance
- **Applicable statute**: state landlord-tenant acts, Fair Housing Act, local housing codes

## Employers & HR

- **EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission)** — for discrimination, harassment, retaliation
- **State Labor Board/Department of Labor** — for wage/hour violations, wrongful termination
- **DOL (US Department of Labor)** — for FLSA violations, FMLA issues
- **OSHA** — for workplace safety
- **Applicable statute**: Title VII, ADA, ADEA, FLSA, FMLA, state employment laws

## Medical Providers & Billing

- **State Medical Board** — for provider conduct
- **HHS Office for Civil Rights** — for HIPAA violations
- **State Attorney General** — for billing fraud or consumer protection
- **State Insurance Commissioner** — when the insurer is the problem, not the provider
- **CMS** — for Medicare/Medicaid billing fraud
- **Applicable statute**: HIPAA, state medical practice acts, No Surprises Act, state billing transparency laws

## Utilities (Electric, Gas, Water, Telecom)

- **State Public Utilities Commission (PUC)** — the primary regulator
- **FCC (Federal Communications Commission)** — for telecom and internet (consumercomplaints.fcc.gov)
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **Applicable statute**: state utility codes, Telecommunications Act

## Government Agencies

- **Inspector General** — every federal agency has one; for waste, fraud, abuse
- **Congressional Representative** — constituent services offices handle agency disputes
- **FOIA (Freedom of Information Act)** — for obtaining records; McKenzie recently filed his first FOIA appeal, describing it as "a very Dangerous Professional letter to an attorney"
- **Ombudsman** — many agencies have one for dispute resolution

## Collections Agencies

- **CFPB** — consumerfinance.gov/complaint
- **FTC** — reportfraud.ftc.gov
- **State Attorney General** — consumer protection division
- **Applicable statute**: Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) — provides statutory damages up to $1,000 per violation. Collectors must validate debts within 5 days of initial contact.

## General Escalation Sequence

When you don't know the specific path, this sequence works broadly:

1. Written complaint to the company (email, then certified mail if no response)
2. Executive escalation (CEO/president email — McKenzie notes these get forwarded with a "?" that triggers urgent attention)
3. State Attorney General consumer complaint
4. Industry-specific regulator (see above)
5. Small claims court (thresholds vary by state, typically $5,000-$10,000)
