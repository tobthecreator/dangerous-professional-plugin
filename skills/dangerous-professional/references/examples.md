# Annotated Examples

Real examples of Dangerous Professional communication drawn from Patrick McKenzie's published work, annotated to show which principles are at play and why each choice works.

## Example 1: Credit Bureau Dispute Letter

From McKenzie's "Identity Theft, Credit Reports, and You" (2017):

> This account is not mine, and the bank has confirmed this -- I am attaching a letter from their SVP to this effect. Please immediately investigate this erroneous tradeline and delete it, or confirm to me your rationale for verifying it in writing. As the bank has acknowledged the error already, if you report to me that it is verified, that will be a per-se violation of the FCRA and I will avail myself of remedies defined in the FCRA or elsewhere.

### Annotation

| Line | Principle |
|-|-|
| "This account is not mine" | States fact, not feeling. No "I believe" or "I think." |
| "the bank has confirmed this" | Uses the institution's own acknowledgment as evidence (Principle 8) |
| "I am attaching a letter from their SVP" | Specificity — names the rank of the person who confirmed. Also signals you got to SVP level. |
| "Please immediately investigate this erroneous tradeline and delete it" | "Require" register — direct instruction, not a request (Principle 5) |
| "or confirm to me your rationale for verifying it in writing" | Asks for written confirmation — paper trail move (Principle 4). Also: if they verify a known error, that act itself is the violation. |
| "per-se violation of the FCRA" | Cites the specific statute naturally, not as a threat but as a statement of legal reality (Principle 6) |
| "I will avail myself of remedies defined in the FCRA or elsewhere" | Strategic ambiguity — doesn't say what remedies, doesn't say lawsuit. "Or elsewhere" is doing enormous work. (Principle 6) |

**Total length**: 3 sentences. The entire letter is under 80 words. Brevity is power (Principle 9).

## Example 2: The Graded-A Communication

McKenzie graded a communication as "A" level and explained why. The sender:

1. Explicitly asked for a postal address to repeat the request via letter
2. Asked which regulator the recipient preferred

### Annotation

**Asking for postal address**: McKenzie notes this "serves no purpose other than to say 'I am keeping, and will in the event of a controversy have developed, a paper trail that will be viewed as accurate.'" The act of asking for the address IS the message.

**Asking which regulator they prefer**: This "suggests the sender is ambivalent to which venue we pursue our differences in because I am confident of my successful execution in any of them." It implies:
- You know multiple regulators exist (institutional literacy)
- You are prepared to file with any of them (competence)
- You are giving them the courtesy of choosing (composed authority)

### Contrast with amateur version

An amateur writes: "Call me or I will sue you." McKenzie notes this signals:
- Lack of professional experience
- No genuine intention toward regulatory action
- No legal counsel involvement

The professional version never mentions suing. It never needs to.

## Example 3: The "What Are My Options?" Incantation

McKenzie published an extended anecdote demonstrating this phrase's power in a bureaucratic context. The key mechanics:

**Why it works as an opening move:**
- It positions you as someone evaluating paths, not someone begging for help
- It forces the representative to enumerate what they CAN do, rather than defending what they won't
- It signals familiarity with how institutions work — you know there are always options
- It's completely non-confrontational, which means the representative has no emotional reason to resist

**Why it works as an escalation move:**
- After a "no," asking "What are my options?" reframes the conversation from closed to open
- It implies the current answer is one possibility, not the final word
- It gives the representative permission to offer alternatives they might not have volunteered

McKenzie identifies this as one of the most powerful single phrases in the Dangerous Professional toolkit.

## Example 4: The Tax Return as Official Document

McKenzie observed that an accepted tax return is one of the most "official" documents available, and that this is an informal truth of the system that professionals can leverage.

His insight: "Tax agencies are broadly supportive of receiving more tax returns. No bureaucrat wants to call the taxman a liar."

**The pattern**: When facing bureaucratic skepticism about your right to do something (operate a business, claim a status), an accepted tax return that reflects that activity creates presumptive legitimacy. Other officials hesitate to contradict what the tax authority has implicitly endorsed by accepting the return.

**The meta-lesson**: The Dangerous Professional looks for documents that carry institutional authority and uses them as leverage in contexts the issuing institution never intended. The document's authority transfers.

## Example 5: The Document Hoarder

McKenzie described a professional interaction where an advisor warned: "You probably won't have all of the documents you need." McKenzie responded that he might be "slightly more sophisticated than the average person you give that disclaimer to."

When tested — "Do you have your 2008 tax returns?" — he could specify: "I filed four returns that year" and offer either the business or personal, Japan or US versions.

**The principle**: Institutions retain factual details across decades. Sophisticated personal record-keeping isn't paranoia — it's the raw material of every successful dispute. The Dangerous Professional keeps everything, organized, accessible, and ready to cite.

## Pattern Summary

Across all examples, the consistent elements are:

1. **Facts, not feelings** — every sentence contains a verifiable claim
2. **Their records, not your narrative** — the strongest evidence is what they already acknowledged
3. **Specific statutes cited naturally** — not as threats, but as context ("this would constitute a per-se violation")
4. **Consequences implied, never stated** — competence is the threat
5. **Brevity** — the credit bureau letter is 3 sentences; the graded-A communication is described in 2 moves
6. **Paper trail creation as an act of communication** — asking for a postal address IS the message
