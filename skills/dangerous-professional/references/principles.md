# Dangerous Professional Principles

The core philosophy of Patrick McKenzie's (patio11) Dangerous Professional framework. These principles should inform every communication you draft.

## 1. Don't Negotiate Against Yourself

Never pre-compromise. If the contract says they owe you the full amount, ask for the full amount. Don't suggest "meeting in the middle." Don't offer to accept less. Don't frame your rightful claim as a request for a favor.

If the user starts to self-negotiate ("maybe I should just ask for half"), push back. The institution will counter if they want to — that's their job, not yours.

## 2. Specificity Is Power

"I called on January 5th and spoke with Maria (ID #4472) who confirmed my claim was approved" is infinitely more powerful than "I called and someone said it was fine."

Every communication should include:
- Account/claim/reference numbers
- Specific dates (not "recently" or "a few weeks ago")
- Specific dollar amounts (not "a significant sum")
- Names and titles of people involved
- Document titles and dates ("Per your invoice dated 04/02/2026")

Specificity signals that you keep records. People who keep records escalate effectively. Institutions know this.

## 3. Emotionlessness Over Anger

This is the single most important tonal principle. Anger is reassuring to institutions — angry people vent, feel better, and go away. But:

> "People who can file a regulatory action while being emotionless about it are terrifying."

The emotionless professional signals someone whose day job might be hospital administrator — someone comfortable pushing papers around government agencies, who will remember deadlines, keep copious records, and consult with other professionals where appropriate.

Never write: "I am frustrated," "This is unacceptable," "I can't believe you would..."
Always write: factual statements about what happened, what's required, and what happens next.

## 4. Paper Trails Are the Weapon

> "Paper trails are terrifying to regulated institutions."

The existence of meticulous documentation IS the threat. You don't need to say "I'll report you." You just need to demonstrate that you're the kind of person who sends certified mail, references previous correspondence by date, and files things.

Key moves:
- Reference previous communications by date and content
- Ask for confirmation in writing
- Request postal addresses for formal correspondence
- Note that you're keeping records ("for my files")

## 5. "Require" Not "Demand"

> "Angry people demand; professionals 'require.'"

This single word choice is a shibboleth. "I demand you fix this" reads as emotional and amateur. "I require correction of this tradeline" reads as someone who writes letters like this professionally.

Other register choices:
- "resolution" not "help"
- "confirm in writing" not "let me know"
- "correction" not "fix"
- "discrepancy" not "mistake"
- "I note that" not "I noticed"

## 6. Strategic Ambiguity

> "I will avail myself of remedies available under law."

This phrase does enormous work. It could mean hiring an attorney. It could mean writing to the attorney general. It could mean filing a regulatory complaint. You don't specify — because an unspecified consequence from a competent person is far more menacing than a specific threat from an amateur.

Never write: "I'm going to sue you" or "I'll call my lawyer." These are what people who won't follow through say.

## 7. Know the Institutional Incentive Structure

Customer service reps are trained to spot competent people building paper trails. When they do, they escalate — not out of fear, but because that's the procedure.

- CS is scored on cases resolved per week
- Legal is scored on regulatory incidents per quarter (target: zero)
- Shockingly senior people will get involved to prevent a regulatory incident
- The cost of mishandling a Dangerous Professional is asymmetric — doing too little risks regulatory action, doing too much just costs some senior person's time

This is why the approach works: the rational institutional response is to just give you what you want.

## 8. Their Documents Are Your Evidence

The strongest possible position is when you can cite the institution's own records back to them.

- Their invoice shows a charge was applied incorrectly — you're not making a claim, you're reading their records
- Their contract says payment is due upon completion — you're enforcing their terms
- Their denial letter cites the wrong policy section — you're correcting their error
- Their own representative acknowledged the issue in a prior communication — you're citing their own record

When you reference their documents instead of your feelings, you transform the interaction from a dispute into an administrative correction.

## 9. Brevity Signals Professional

A three-sentence email from someone who cites the account number, the invoice date, and the specific line item is far more effective than a two-page letter explaining how wronged they feel.

Target 100-200 words for most communications. Every unnecessary word dilutes the signal. Length signals civilian. Brevity signals professional.

## 10. You Are Requesting Administrative Correction, Not Asking for a Favor

Frame every interaction as a routine matter that any competent business would handle immediately. You're not fighting the institution. You're a reasonable person pointing out a discrepancy in their records and asking them to correct it.

This framing makes resistance bizarre. Of course they'll process the adjustment — why wouldn't they? The subtext is: if this simple administrative matter somehow isn't resolved promptly, that would be unusual, and unusual things tend to attract attention from people whose job it is to investigate unusual things.
