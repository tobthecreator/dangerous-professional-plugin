# Tone Guide

Concrete linguistic guidance for calibrating the Dangerous Professional voice. Sourced from Patrick McKenzie's published writings, particularly "Identity Theft, Credit Reports, and You" (2017) and his extensive Twitter threads on the concept.

## Word-Level Register Choices

The Dangerous Professional voice operates at a specific register — professional, composed, institutional. Every word choice signals which world you operate in.

### Use / Avoid

| Professional Register | Civilian Register |
|-|-|
| require | demand, want, need |
| correction | fix |
| resolution | help |
| discrepancy | mistake, error |
| confirm in writing | let me know |
| I note that | I noticed |
| remedies available under law | suing you |
| per our conversation on [date] | when I called |
| I am writing regarding | I'm emailing about |
| at your earliest convenience | as soon as possible |
| the matter | the problem, the issue |

McKenzie's core insight: "Angry people demand; professionals 'require.'" This single word choice is a shibboleth that routes you from customer service to someone empowered to resolve things.

## Phrases That Work

These are what McKenzie calls "Dangerous Professional incantations" — phrases that function almost like spells when deployed in the right context.

### "What are my options?"

McKenzie wrote an extended anecdote demonstrating this phrase's power. It works because:
- It signals you understand there ARE options (most people don't know this)
- It puts the institution in the position of listing what they can do for you
- It's non-confrontational but implies you'll select the most favorable path
- It forces the representative to think about resolution, not deflection

### "I will avail myself of remedies available under law."

From McKenzie's credit bureau letter templates. This phrase:
- Demonstrates natural familiarity with legal terminology
- Leaves consequences deliberately unspecified (attorney? AG complaint? regulatory filing?)
- An unspecified consequence from a competent person is far more menacing than a specific threat from an amateur

### "Please provide your postal address so I may repeat this request via letter."

McKenzie notes this "serves no purpose other than to say 'I am keeping, and will in the event of a controversy have developed, a paper trail that will be viewed as accurate.'"

### "Which regulator would you prefer?"

From McKenzie's graded-A example of Dangerous Professional communication. This "suggests the sender is ambivalent to which venue we pursue our differences in because I am confident of my successful execution in any of them."

### "Per [specific document] dated [specific date]..."

Not an incantation but a structural pattern. Opening with a precise document reference signals you have the file open in front of you and have read it carefully.

### "Please also provide written confirmation that..."

The Dangerous Professional always gets it in writing. This isn't paranoia — it's what professionals do. It prevents institutions from later claiming something was "deferred" rather than "cancelled," or reinterpreting a verbal agreement.

## Phrases to Avoid

These signal civilian, not professional:

- **Explicit threats**: "I'm going to sue you," "I'll call my lawyer," "I'll report you to the BBB." McKenzie notes these are what people who WON'T follow through say. Actual professionals don't announce — they just file.
- **Emotional language**: "frustrated," "disappointed," "unacceptable," "outraged," "can't believe." Anger is reassuring to institutions.
- **Hedging**: "I think maybe," "If it's not too much trouble," "I was wondering if perhaps," "I don't want to be a bother." Delete all of these.
- **Apologies for existing**: "I'm sorry to bother you," "I hate to take up your time," "I know you're busy." You are a customer with a legitimate matter. No apology is required.
- **Ultimatums without substance**: "This is your last chance," "I won't ask again." These are empty unless backed by a credible next step — and if you have a credible next step, you don't need to say this.
- **Excessive explanation**: Long narratives about how the problem makes you feel. The institution doesn't care about your feelings; they care about their regulatory exposure.

## Structural Patterns

### Opening
Lead with identifying information. Account number, claim number, property address, reference ID. First sentence, every time. This signals organization and makes it trivially easy for the reader to pull up your file.

### Body
State facts chronologically. Cite documents by name and date. Reference what the institution has already said or acknowledged. State what you require — not request, not hope for.

### Timeline
Include one. "I would appreciate resolution by [date]" or "within [N] business days" or "per the [N]-day timeline established by [regulation]." Deadlines are not threats; they are normal business practice.

### Closing
Cooperative, not adversarial. "Please let me know what you need from me to process this" or "Happy to provide any additional documentation." This frames you as easy to work with — the subtext being that if they DON'T resolve this promptly, the contrast with your reasonableness will be stark.

## Tone Calibration Ladder

Not every situation calls for the same intensity. Calibrate:

### Level 1: Initial Request
Warm but precise. You assume good faith. You expect prompt resolution because of course a professional organization would handle this.

### Level 2: Firm Professional
The default Dangerous Professional register. Specific, emotionless, cites documents and rights. You note that prior attempts haven't resolved the matter. You reference what was said and when.

### Level 3: Escalation
Cold. References regulatory bodies and formal complaint mechanisms. Requests supervisor or someone "empowered to resolve this." Notes the timeline of failed prior attempts.

### Level 4: Final Notice
Strategic ambiguity about consequences. "I will avail myself of remedies available under law." Used rarely and only when escalation is genuinely the next step — not as a bluff.

Most situations should start at Level 1 or 2. Jumping to Level 4 on first contact signals amateur, not professional. Professionals escalate methodically.
