# Gotchas

Real failure modes when Claude drafts Dangerous Professional communications. These are specific, non-obvious, and drawn from how the approach actually breaks down. Check every draft against this list.

## Claude writes too aggressively

The Dangerous Professional NEVER threatens explicitly. "I will contact my attorney" is amateur. "I will avail myself of remedies available under law" is professional. The difference is strategic ambiguity — the professional leaves consequences open-ended because an unspecified consequence from a competent person is more menacing than a specific one from an amateur. If the draft contains any explicit threat, rewrite it.

## Claude writes too long

McKenzie's credit bureau dispute letter is 3 sentences and under 80 words. Most Dangerous Professional communications should be 100-200 words. If the draft exceeds 200 words, something is wrong — either you're including unnecessary narrative, explaining feelings, or adding qualifications that dilute the signal. Length is the single clearest civilian marker. Cut ruthlessly.

## Claude negotiates against the user

This is the most insidious failure. Claude will sometimes suggest the user "consider accepting" a partial resolution, or frame a full claim as "ambitious." It will add softeners like "if you're open to a compromise" or "you might consider meeting them halfway." This is exactly what McKenzie warns against — never negotiate against yourself. The institution will counter if they want to. That's their job, not yours or the user's.

## Claude uses emotional language

Words like "frustrated," "disappointed," "unacceptable," "outraged," and "can't believe" signal civilian. McKenzie's core tonal principle: "You're not angry at either the bank or anyone working at it." Anger is REASSURING to institutions because angry people vent and leave. Emotionless competence is terrifying because it signals someone who will methodically follow through. Strip all emotional language from every draft.

## Claude adds unnecessary apologies

"I'm sorry to bother you," "I hate to take up your time," "I understand you're busy," "I don't want to be difficult." Delete all of these. You are a customer or counterparty with a legitimate matter. McKenzie explicitly warns against minimizing "your own experience, competence, success, or chances." An apology for existing signals you don't believe you deserve resolution.

## Claude forgets to cite specific documents

The single most important signal of competence is specificity. If the user has provided documents (contracts, invoices, letters, emails), the draft MUST reference them by name, date, and content. "Per your invoice dated [date]" is the minimum. If the draft says "your records" instead of "your invoice dated April 2, 2026, Account ID PR771013," it has failed. Always ask the user for documents before drafting.

## Claude cites regulations incorrectly

If you're not certain of a statute number, section, or specific regulatory provision, do NOT cite it specifically. It is better to write "applicable consumer protection statutes" or "remedies available under the FCRA" than to cite a wrong section number. A wrong citation signals amateur research, which is worse than no citation at all. Only cite specific sections when you can verify them.

## Claude adds generic legal boilerplate

"This letter shall serve as formal notice..." / "Please be advised that..." / "Failure to respond within the statutory period will result in..." These are templates from free legal letter generators. They signal someone who googled "how to write a demand letter." The Dangerous Professional voice is conversational and composed, not legalistic. Write like a competent person who happens to know the law, not like someone performing a legal role.

## Claude formats as a formal business letter

Unless the user specifically needs certified mail or a printed letter, the output should be a plain email. "Dear Sir or Madam" with a block format and a signature block is overkill for most situations. Match the medium. If it's an email, write an email. If it's going via certified mail, a more formal format is appropriate. Ask the user about the medium if unclear.

## Claude doesn't ask for documents first

The strongest Dangerous Professional move is citing the institution's own records back to them. If Claude drafts without first asking what documents the user has, it's leaving the most powerful leverage on the table. The interview step (Step 1-2 in the skill) exists for a reason — never skip it under time pressure. A draft grounded in their documents is worth 10x a draft grounded in the user's narrative alone.

## Claude over-explains the strategy

The draft should speak for itself. Don't include parenthetical notes like "(this signals competence)" or "(this creates a paper trail)" in the actual email. The user should understand the strategy from the separate strategic note — but the email itself should read as natural, composed professional correspondence, not as a document annotated with persuasion tactics.
